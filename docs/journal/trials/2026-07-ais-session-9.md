## Verdict: the honest negative — no economic signal in this cohort,
## and the pipeline proved itself by NOT manufacturing one

- All 17 stops verified and classified: **4 vessels left the region**
  (confirmed/probable departure tracks, incl. one small craft tracked
  mid-Pacific to Maui at a physically plausible 3.2kn), **6 re-tasked**
  to different routes (two container ships confirmed redeployed
  Pacific→Atlantic), **6 apparent lay-ups** (3 abrupt, 3
  cadence-decay), **1 government reassignment**. Zero renames (M1),
  zero multi-emitter artifacts (M2), zero endpoint coverage losses.
- **The route-side/economic-signal shortlist is essentially empty**:
  every endpoint remained heavily trafficked by other operators after
  the subject vessel stopped. The LA/LB↔Oakland cluster looked like a
  4-vessel route collapse and resolved into independent redeployments
  off a route ~40 other operators kept running. The two weakest
  conjectural candidates (a Neuse River NC marina-ish endpoint, an
  Indian River Inlet DE dredging-support endpoint) are explicitly
  flagged as individual-operator dropouts, not industry signals.
- Interpretation for the thesis: not a refutation — a calibration. The
  2024–2025 window and a 17-vessel mostly-small-craft cohort is thin
  ground for macro signals; the ~18 further years are where the
  indicator idea gets its real test. What this session established is
  the **verification pipeline** that makes any future positive
  believable: a positive that survives M10 means something.
- **M10 stop-verification checklist** recorded as a pattern (identity →
  emitter → terminal-trajectory read → endpoint-continuity → own-vessel
  seasonality baseline), plus a new terminal signature
  (accelerating-offshore-through-a-strait = departure, not lay-up) and
  a third documented failure mode for the m2-gray-zone caveat
  (long-transit false positives).
- Cadence-decay vs abrupt-halt was recorded per vessel — the
  distinction the expert's economic reading needs.
