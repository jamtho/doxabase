severitycaveat
● SevereThe view nullifies IMO0000000, vessel_type=0, and length/width=0 before comparing years (see caveat/placeholder-encoding-shift-2025) and strips literal quotes from names (see caveat/name-quoting-artifact). Removing these steps floods the classifier with tens of thousands of fake identity-loss events at 2025-01-01.

● ModerateBecause this feed canonicalizes identity annually (see caveat/identity-year-constant), M1 cannot say WHEN within the window a rename or reassignment happened - only that the 2024 identity differs from the 2025 identity. M1 method: classify year-boundary identity changes by corroboration (spelling variant vs rename vs MMSI reassignment).

https://ais.study/pattern/method-m1-identity-change-classifier
WHAT IT DETECTS: for each MMSI active in both 2024 and 2025, whether its identity change across the year boundary is (a) upstream spelling re-canonicalization of the same hull, (b) a genuine rename of the same hull, or (c) a different physical vessel taking over the MMSI. WHY IT WORKS: this feed freezes identity per year, so the boundary is the only place identity can change; and the corroborating fields fail independently - a rename keeps call sign/IMO/dimensions, while a reassignment changes them together. Name similarity (containment, levenshtein<=2, jaro-winkler>=0.88) absorbs the upstream re-canonicalization noise before corroboration is consulted. HOW TO APPLY: run the SQL on view year-boundary-identity-changes; triage suspected_mmsi_reassignment (169 rows) and rename_same_hull (1,288) by activity days; treat ambiguous_name_change as a review queue, not a result.

M2 method: detect shared/cloned MMSIs by persistent impossible speeds with wide geographic span; separate GPS jitter by span.

https://ais.study/pattern/method-m2-multi-emitter-detector
WHAT IT DETECTS: MMSIs carried by more than one physical transmitter at the same time (cloned, misprogrammed, default, or infrastructure MMSIs), using only the cheap index layer. WHY IT WORKS: two simultaneous emitters produce inter-fix jumps between their locations, so max_inter_msg_speed_ms is impossibly high on essentially EVERY shared day (persistence), and the daily bounding box stays wide (separation). A single vessel with a broken GPS also produces impossible speeds but inside a tight box - so median conflict-day span >= 1 deg splits the two. HOW TO APPLY: run the view SQL; multi_emitter rows are near-certain shared MMSIs (top ranks confirmed by broadcast clustering); follow up each hit with broadcast-level position clustering + transceiver class to enumerate the emitters. Round-number MMSIs (X00000000, 338000000) deserve a strong prior of being shared defaults.

M3 method: segment per-vessel silences into in-place power-downs, coverage-exit voyages, and implausible jumps, using outage-corrected gaps and centroid displacement.

https://ais.study/pattern/method-m3-silence-gap-segmentation
WHAT IT DETECTS: every multi-day silence per MMSI, classified by what the vessel did while unheard: silent_in_place (berth power-down / lay-up), coverage_exit_voyage (sailed beyond the terrestrial network: ocean transit or foreign call), implausible_jump (>30 kn implied: clone or data fault), local_gap. WHY IT WORKS: at day granularity a silence is fully characterized by (duration, endpoint displacement); implied speed = displacement/duration separates continuous sailing (3-25 kn) from teleports; the vessel's duty ratio separates meaningful silence from Class B lifestyle gaps; subtracting the 2024-08-26 outage prevents a systematic false gap across the whole fleet. WHAT IT IS NOT: a dark-ship detector - in a terrestrial feed, most silence is innocent range loss (see caveat/m3-silence-is-not-dark). USES: voyage segmentation (ocean legs), lay-up windows for dating identity changes (paired with M1), residual-anomaly triage.

M4 method: read cargo operations (load/discharge events, terminals, laden/ballast cycles) from day-over-day draft changes.

https://ais.study/pattern/method-m4-draft-cargo-events
WHAT IT DETECTS: cargo-operation events and terminal geography for draft-disciplined fleets (tankers first): each day-over-day draught increase >=1 m is a loading, each decrease a discharge; modal position clusters of many events identify the vessel's load and discharge terminals; the alternation gives the laden/ballast rhythm and voyage counts. WHY IT WORKS: draught is the one static field tied to physics - a tanker MUST change draft when it moves thousands of tonnes, and crews of working tankers do maintain it (188-248 events per active tanker here). WHY IT PARTIALLY FAILS: the update is manual and often entered en route, so single events smear along the transit lane - always aggregate cycles before naming a terminal (see caveats). HOW TO APPLY: run the view SQL (adjust vessel_types and min delta); aggregate by event type and rounded position; read trades as load-cluster -> discharge-cluster pairs.

M5 method: detect operational vessel relationships (convoys, project fleets, tug pairs) via persistent same-day co-location while moving, discriminated by midpoint mobility.

https://ais.study/pattern/method-m5-co-movement-pairs
WHAT IT DETECTS: pairs (and, transitively, fleets) of vessels that WORK TOGETHER: they are within 3 km on many days when both genuinely move (>20 km/day), and the places where they meet wander over long distances (mobility >=100 km). WHY IT WORKS: chance co-location concentrates in fixed places (harbors, ferry lanes); an operational tie is the only common reason two movers keep meeting at ever-new locations. The mobility statistic (spread of pair midpoints across pair-days) encodes that distinction in one number. HOW TO APPLY: run the view SQL (grid self-join, ~1.5 s for two years); read pairs with high pair_days and mobility as ties; confirm important ones at broadcast level; cluster overlapping pairs into fleets. TUNING: lower pair_days to ~30 and mobility to ~50 km for seasonal ties; raise the 3 km radius for offshore escort work.
