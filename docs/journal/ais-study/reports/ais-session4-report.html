● ModerateThe index stores per-day centroids only; a vessel that moved a lot on its last active day has a centroid biased away from its true last position (up to ~half the day's track). Displacements are therefore +-100 km fuzzy for fast movers; use broadcast last/first fixes (via base_date_time, not the buggy timestamp) when a gap matters. ● ModerateLeisure and small commercial craft power their transponders on only when used; their gap distribution says nothing about behaviour of interest. The view exposes duty (active_days/span_days) so consumers can filter (duty >= 0.85 recommended) or baseline per vessel; do not run anomaly triage on low-duty MMSIs.
● ModerateM3 classifies coverage_exit_voyage purely by point-to-point centroid displacement (>=300 km at plausible speed), with no reference to where the receiver network actually is. Cross-referencing M3 output against the AIS Base Stations receiver map (AISBaseStation.gpkg, 290 stations) shows two concrete failure directions. (1) Over-calling: MMSI 100000000's repeated ~400-436 km Gulf-coast gaps (Mississippi River mouth to Pensacola/Panama City FL) have BOTH endpoints only 19-22 nm from the nearest NAIS station -- well inside the ~40-50 nm coverage envelope (caveat/m3-silence-is-not-dark) -- yet score coverage_exit_voyage purely because the coastline transit exceeds 300 km. (2) Under-calling: a cluster of short/malformed-MMSI records (mmsi 211, 407, 527, 621-634, 809-832 among others) shows repeated 135-298 km gaps with BOTH endpoints already 270-360 nm from the nearest station (open North Atlantic east of Georges Bank) -- already outside the envelope on both ends -- yet these score local_gap because displacement stays under 300 km. The GOLDEN BEAR worked example (claim e9d46474) is a case where the rule happens to work because its endpoints (Guam, Strait of Juan de Fuca) are both near-coast/near-station. A geometry-aware version would check each gap endpoint's (or the straight-line path's) distance to the nearest receiver rather than relying on raw endpoint-to-endpoint displacement.
Impact: coverage_exit_voyage should not be read as 'left the network' without checking the endpoints against the receiver map first -- some long coastal transits (over-called) never left coverage and their silence is unexplained; some already-far-offshore small-displacement gaps (under-called) are filed as undifferentiated local_gap when they are, in fact, ordinary coverage loss that could be reclassified with more confidence than the local_gap bucket implies.
● SevereThis is a terrestrial-receiver feed covering US coasts plus Alaska, Hawaii, Guam and Puerto Rico. A vessel sailing beyond receiver range goes silent with its transponder on; most large-displacement gaps are ocean transits (coverage_exit_voyage), not dark activity. True AIS-off detection at sea is impossible from this data alone; silent_in_place events at a berth are usually routine power-downs. Treat gap classes as descriptive segmentation, and only escalate residual anomalies (e.g. implausible_jump, or in-coverage displacements inconsistent with the vessel's habits).

M1 method: classify year-boundary identity changes by corroboration (spelling variant vs rename vs MMSI reassignment).

https://ais.study/pattern/method-m1-identity-change-classifier
WHAT IT DETECTS: for each MMSI active in both 2024 and 2025, whether its identity change across the year boundary is (a) upstream spelling re-canonicalization of the same hull, (b) a genuine rename of the same hull, or (c) a different physical vessel taking over the MMSI. M3 method: segment per-vessel silences into in-place power-downs, coverage-exit voyages, and implausible jumps, using outage-corrected gaps and centroid displacement.

https://ais.study/pattern/method-m3-silence-gap-segmentation
WHAT IT DETECTS: every multi-day silence per MMSI, classified by what the vessel did while unheard: silent_in_place (berth power-down / lay-up), coverage_exit_voyage (sailed beyond the terrestrial network: ocean transit or foreign call), implausible_jump (>30 kn implied: clone or data fault), local_gap. WHY IT WORKS: at day granularity a silence is fully characterized by (duration, endpoint displacement); implied speed = displacement/duration separates continuous sailing (3-25 kn) from teleports; the vessel's duty ratio separates meaningful silence from Class B lifestyle gaps; subtracting the 2024-08-26 outage prevents a systematic false gap across the whole fleet. WHAT IT IS NOT: a dark-ship detector - in a terrestrial feed, most silence is innocent range loss (see caveat/m3-silence-is-not-dark). USES: voyage segmentation (ocean legs), lay-up windows for dating identity changes (paired with M1), residual-anomaly triage.

M4 method: read cargo operations (load/discharge events, terminals, laden/ballast cycles) from day-over-day draft changes.

https://ais.study/pattern/method-m4-draft-cargo-events
WHAT IT DETECTS: cargo-operation events and terminal geography for draft-disciplined fleets (tankers first): each day-over-day draught increase >=1 m is a loading, each decrease a discharge; modal position clusters of many events identify the vessel's load and discharge terminals; the alternation gives the laden/ballast rhythm and voyage counts. WHY IT WORKS: draught is the one static field tied to physics - a tanker MUST change draft when it moves thousands of tonnes, and crews of working tankers do maintain it (188-248 events per active tanker here). HOW TO APPLY: run the view SQL (adjust vessel_types and min delta); aggregate by event type and rounded position; read trades as load-cluster -> discharge-cluster pairs.
